## Introduction

Every civilization left a signature in stone. From the geometry of Giza to the terraces of Machu Picchu, these monuments are the surviving sentences of vanished worlds — ambitious, technical, and unmistakably human. This collection reads them side by side, across continents and millennia.

## Key assets

- **Pyramids of Giza** (Egypt) — Only surviving Ancient Wonder; Old Kingdom royal tombs and the Great Sphinx.
- **Great Wall of China** (China) — Continuous defensive fortifications begun in the 7th century BCE; the most recognized monument of imperial China.
- **Colosseum** (Italy) — Roman amphitheatre (80 CE), the largest ever built and the enduring emblem of imperial Rome.
- **Persepolis** (Iran) — Ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Persian Empire.
- **Acropolis of Athens** (Greece) — Classical citadel crowned by the Parthenon, the architectural ideal of Western antiquity.
- **Angkor Wat** (Cambodia) — Largest religious monument in the world, a 12th-century Khmer temple-city.
- **Machu Picchu** (Peru) — 15th-century Inca citadel in the Andes, an icon of pre-Columbian engineering and one of the New Seven Wonders.
- **Terracotta Army** (China) — Thousands of life-size clay warriors guarding the tomb of China's first emperor.
- **Teotihuacan** (Mexico) — Vast Mesoamerican city of the Pyramids of the Sun and Moon.

## Educational narrative

Placed together, the great civilizations reveal a shared grammar: the urge to align with the heavens, to honour the dead, to project power and to organize labour at vast scale. Giza and Teotihuacan never met, yet both reached for the sun; Rome and the Han built walls and roads to bind empires; the Khmer and the Inca shaped mountains and forests into sacred order. The collection invites comparison rather than hierarchy — a single human achievement seen in many tongues.
